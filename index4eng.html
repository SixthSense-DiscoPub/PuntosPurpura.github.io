Miths and legends

Features

The White Lady is a famous ghost who appears at Borley Rectory, considered “the most haunted house in England.” It is said that this female apparition wanders the hallways, foretelling tragedies and terrifying those who see her.
The legend of the Witch of Endor tells that this sorceress could communicate with the dead using her magical powers. Her iconic pointed hat symbolizes witchcraft and connection to the spiritual world.
In the Celtic tradition of Samhain, the predecessor of Halloween, pumpkins were used to ward off evil spirits. This practice remains alive in Irish and Scottish culture, linking the pumpkin to protection against dark forces.
In Scottish mythology, there is talk of a giant serpent that lived in Fingal’s Cave. This mystical creature guarded ancient secrets and was the protagonist of numerous stories about battles with local heroes.
The ravens Huginn and Muninn were the messengers of the god Odin, carrying information from the world of the living and the dead. In European culture, the raven symbolizes connection with the afterlife and is associated with omens and hidden wisdom.
The witches of Zugarramurdi were persecuted in the 17th century for alleged witches’ sabbaths. The broomstick is their classic symbol, representing their nocturnal rituals and the legend of flying to meet the devil.
Werewolves are legendary figures in Europe, representing humans who transform into wolves during the full moon. In many cultures, it was believed that this transformation could be caused by a spell or a bite. Over the centuries, stories about werewolves have been passed down and adapted, influencing literature and popular folklore, keeping the mystery and terror surrounding these creatures alive.
Sweeney Todd, the infamous barber of Fleet Street, is a London urban legend. It is said that his spirit still roams the dark streets seeking revenge. At night, some claim to hear the sound of a knife and chilling whispers near old street lamps, as if the ghostly barber is waiting for his next victim.
